# Copy Playbooks — LinkedIn Profile Perfector

> These playbooks are **self-contained and portable.** They do not depend on
> any external skill or private repository. Every rule, formula, and example
> below is embedded here so the skill works on any machine that clones this repo.

---

## 1. Embedded Copy Rules

Apply ALL of the following rules to every piece of copy produced — headline,
About, experience, and skills. No exceptions.

### 1.1 Core Writing Rules

| Rule | What It Means |
|------|---------------|
| **Short & concise** | Every sentence earns its place. Cut anything that does not add information, proof, or keyword signal. |
| **Keyword-rich** | Target keywords must appear in the headline, the first 100 chars of About, and at least one bullet per role. Use exact-match phrases — LinkedIn search is literal. |
| **Bullets for every experience role** | No dense paragraph descriptions. Every role gets 4–6 short achievement bullets. Bullets are faster to scan, more keyword-indexable, and more memorable than prose. |
| **Benefits over features** | Write from the reader's perspective. "Reduced customer onboarding time by 40%" (benefit they get) beats "Responsible for onboarding improvements" (feature you have). |
| **Active voice** | Start bullets and sentences with strong verbs. "Led," "Built," "Launched," "Grew," "Reduced" — not "Was responsible for" or "Involved in." |
| **Metrics wherever possible** | Numbers earn credibility. Surface percentages, dollar amounts, headcount, time savings, scale (users, SKUs, markets), and growth rates **that already appear in the user's material** — never invent them (see Anti-Fabrication Rule below). Where a metric would strengthen a line but isn't in the source, insert a bracketed prompt like `[add metric: e.g., % growth]` for the user to fill. Only convert a number to a range ("3–5×") or relative comparison ("largest in department") when the underlying figure came from the user. |

### 1.0 Anti-Fabrication Rule — Highest Priority

> **Never introduce a metric, employer, job title, date, client name, dollar
> figure, or achievement that is not present in the user's provided material.**
> The rewrite step optimizes how true facts are *expressed* — it does not
> manufacture new ones. When a stronger line clearly needs a number the source
> doesn't contain, leave a bracketed placeholder (`[add metric: …]`,
> `[add client name]`, `[add timeframe]`) for the user to complete. A profile
> that invents accomplishments is worse than one that is merely under-optimized:
> it exposes the user to real reputational and hiring risk. This rule overrides
> every other copy rule, including "Metrics wherever possible."

> **The worked examples throughout this file are illustrative.** Their specific
> numbers ("$50M monthly," "82% → 94%," "10M+ daily requests") demonstrate
> *structure and voice* — do not copy them into a real profile or pattern-match
> them onto a user whose material lacks comparable figures.

### 1.2 Cut List — Remove Every Instance

**Weak intensifiers** — these add no information and dilute credibility:

- "really" → cut entirely or replace with a specific qualifier
- "very" → cut entirely or replace with a measurable modifier
- "just" → cut entirely (almost always filler)
- "quite" → cut entirely
- "extremely" → replace with a metric or delete

**AI-sounding filler** — signals generated copy; hurts credibility and authenticity:

- "I hope this finds you well" → delete
- "I'd love to" → replace with direct intent ("I'm happy to", "reach me at")
- "leverage" → replace with "use," "apply," or the specific action
- "synergy" → replace with the specific collaboration or outcome
- "passionate about" → replace with a demonstration ("I've spent 8 years building…")
- "results-driven professional" → delete; show the results instead
- "dynamic" → delete; specifics outperform adjectives
- "detail-oriented" → delete unless you have a specific proof point
- "hardworking" → delete; everyone says this; only proof matters
- "team player" → delete; show cross-functional collaboration as a bullet
- "thought leader" → never self-apply; let recommendations and content prove it
- "innovative" → delete; describe the specific innovation instead
- "seasoned" → replace with tenure or scope ("12 years," "led 4 product cycles")
- "unique" → delete; specifics create differentiation without claiming it

### 1.3 Experience Rewrite Format

Every experience role rewrite follows this exact structure:

```
**[Job Title]** · [Company] · [Start Date – End Date] · [Location or Remote]

[2–3 sentence role summary: scope, team size, key responsibility, context]

• [Active verb] + [result or metric] + [keyword where natural]
• [Active verb] + [result or metric] + [keyword where natural]
• [Active verb] + [result or metric] + [keyword where natural]
• [Active verb] + [result or metric] + [keyword where natural]
• [Active verb] + [result or metric] + [keyword where natural]
```

Present each role as a **Current → Optimized** pair so the user sees exactly
what changed and can verify accuracy before pasting.

## 2. Headline Playbook

### 2.1 The Limit

**220 characters maximum.** LinkedIn truncates headlines in search results at
~220 characters. Headlines that run over lose their value proposition in the
single most visible surface on the platform.

### 2.2 The Formula

```
[Role] | [Key Expertise] | [Value Proposition]
```

The three-part structure with pipe separators is the most battle-tested
LinkedIn headline format. Each segment serves a distinct function:

| Segment | Function | Character budget |
|---------|----------|-----------------|
| **Role** | Tells the algorithm and the reader what you are; anchor keyword | ~40 chars |
| **Key Expertise** | Adds specificity; secondary and tertiary keywords; tools or methods | ~60 chars |
| **Value Proposition** | Differentiates; answers "so what?"; includes proof or outcome | ~80 chars |

### 2.3 Weak vs. Strong Examples

**WEAK — causes a skip:**

| Weak Headline | Why It Fails |
|---------------|-------------|
| "Looking for opportunities" | No value prop; signals passivity; invisible in search |
| "Unemployed Product Manager" | Never lead with a gap; zero keyword signal; no differentiation |
| "Student at University" | No professional value; no keyword hook; nothing to click on |
| "Open to work" | Passivity signal; LinkedIn's Open-to-Work badge does this job; the headline should do more |

**STRONG — drives clicks:**

| Strong Headline | Why It Works |
|----------------|-------------|
| "Senior Product Manager \| B2B SaaS \| Driving 0→1 Products from Concept to $10M ARR" | Role + domain + specific proof point with dollar metric |
| "Data Scientist \| Machine Learning & Analytics \| Turning Data into Business Decisions" | Clean triad; high-search keywords; outcome-oriented end |
| "Software Engineer \| Python, AWS, Kubernetes \| Building Scalable Systems at Fortune 500" | Tool keywords for ATS + seniority signal in last segment |
| "Marketing Director \| Growth & Brand Strategy \| 3× Revenue Growth at Series B Startups" | Level + function + quantified result + stage context |

### 2.4 Keyword Strategy

**Where to find headline keywords:**

1. Target job descriptions — the 3–5 words that appear most frequently
2. LinkedIn's skill suggestions (when editing your Skills section, LinkedIn auto-suggests high-volume terms)
3. Profiles of 5–10 people already in your target role — note repeating terms
4. Industry publications and conference tracks in your space — recurrent vocabulary signals search volume
5. Your own certifications and credentials — these are exact-match search terms

**What to include:**

- Primary job title + common variations (e.g., "Product Manager" and "PM" count differently)
- Key skills, tools, or methodologies the target audience searches for
- Industry-specific terms (e.g., "PLG," "Series B," "B2B SaaS," "Fractional CFO")
- Certifications that have standalone search value (CPA, CFA, PMP, AWS Certified)

**What to avoid:**

- Vague superlatives ("Top-ranked," "Award-winning") without named proof
- Company names in the headline (the Experience section handles this)
- Buzzwords from the Cut List above
- Stacking too many pipes — three segments is the max before it becomes unreadable

### 2.5 Per-Mode Headline Variants

#### Mode: Job Search

**Priority:** keyword density, ATS discoverability, target title prominence.

- Put the exact target role title first — recruiters filter by title; it must match
- Include 2–3 high-volume skills or tools the role requires
- Value prop can be a key metric or a scope signal ("Fortune 500," "enterprise," "0→1")
- Avoid emotional language; lean factual and specific
- Example: `Senior Product Manager | B2B SaaS, Agile, Roadmaps | 3× ARR Growth in 18 Months`

#### Mode: Creator / Grow Profile

**Priority:** value proposition for followers — what they get by following you.

- Lead with what you teach, share, or create (not just your job title)
- Frame around the audience's gain: "I share weekly frameworks for..." or "Follow for..."
- Include the niche keyword that your target follower would search
- Example: `Founder & PM Coach | Product Strategy & Career Growth | Weekly Frameworks for 50K+ Builders`

#### Mode: Consulting / Win Clients

**Priority:** outcome clarity, client result prominence, offer specificity.

- Lead with the result you deliver for clients, not your internal title
- Include the client type and the problem you solve
- Add a specific proof element (client count, outcome metric, named vertical)
- Example: `B2B Revenue Consultant | GTM Strategy & Sales Systems | Helped 40+ SaaS Companies Hit $1M ARR`

#### Mode: Personal Branding

**Priority:** point-of-view, memorability, what you stand for.

- Lead with your area of authority or your distinctive perspective
- Include your primary content topic or expertise claim
- End with a position statement — what you're building, fighting for, or changing
- Example: `Chief Product Officer | Future of AI-Assisted Work | Building Human-First Products at Scale`

## 3. About Playbook

### 3.1 Limits and Targets

| Constraint | Value |
|------------|-------|
| Hard character limit | 2,600 characters |
| Recommended range | 1,500–2,000 characters |
| Recommended paragraph count | 3–5 paragraphs |
| "See more" threshold (visible without clicking) | ~300 characters (~3 lines on desktop) |

The first ~300 characters are the **highest-value real estate** on the entire
profile. They appear in search results, in recruiter inboxes, and as the
visible preview before the "see more" click. If those 300 characters don't
create a pull, a significant portion of visitors never read the rest.

### 3.2 Structure

```
[HOOK — 1–2 sentences, ~100–150 chars, creates pull or states the value prop]

[WHO YOU ARE + WHAT YOU DO — 2–4 sentences, who you serve, for how long, context]

[KEY ACHIEVEMENTS + SPECIALTIES — 3–5 bullet points or a short narrative list]

[WHAT YOU OFFER OR SEEK — 2–3 sentences, mode-specific: service, role, or platform pitch]

[SKILLS / KEYWORDS LIST — comma-separated, 10–15 terms, for SEO and skim readability]

[CALL TO ACTION — 1 sentence + contact method]
```

### 3.3 Full Worked Examples by Mode

#### Job Search / Personal Branding (combined template)

> **Style template — use this structure and tone:**

```
I help SaaS companies turn product ideas into revenue.

For the past 8 years, I've been building products people actually want to use.
From a payments platform that processed $50M monthly to a developer tool used
by 100K+ engineers, I've led cross-functional teams from idea to launch.

What I do best:
→ Transform ambiguous customer problems into clear product roadmaps
→ Build and lead high-performing product teams
→ Drive growth through data-informed decisions

Key skills: Product Strategy, Roadmap Planning, Agile/Scrum, User Research,
A/B Testing, SQL, B2B SaaS

Let's connect — I'm always happy to chat about product, SaaS, or career advice.
Reach me at [email].
```

**What makes this work:** The hook is a crisp mission statement, not a bio.
The achievements are shown, not claimed. The skills section is a keyword block
that's both human-readable and search-indexed. The CTA is warm and specific.

#### Consulting / Win Clients

```
If your pipeline is full but deals keep stalling — I fix that.

I'm a B2B sales and GTM consultant who has worked with 35+ SaaS companies across
Series A–C. I specialize in diagnosing conversion breakdowns and building the
sales systems, playbooks, and team structures that turn them around.

Recent client results:
→ Took a fintech startup from $800K to $3.2M ARR in 14 months
→ Rebuilt the enterprise motion at a data infrastructure company — 2× deal size
→ Reduced sales cycle from 90 to 38 days at a vertical SaaS company

If you're building a GTM team, launching into enterprise, or unstuck from a
plateau — reach out. I work with 3–5 clients at a time.

Expertise: B2B SaaS Sales, GTM Strategy, Sales Enablement, Revenue Operations,
CRM Implementation, Outbound Sequences, Enterprise Sales, Fractional VP of Sales

Book a 20-minute call: [calendar link] or email [email]
```

**What makes this work:** The opener is a problem-statement the right client
recognises immediately. The results are specific and verifiable. The offer
is scoped (3–5 clients), which signals selectivity and drives urgency.

#### Creator / Grow Profile

```
Every week I break down what's actually working in B2B content marketing —
no fluff, no hot takes, just tested frameworks.

I've spent 10 years on both sides of the content table: leading content teams
at B2B SaaS companies and running a consultancy for 50+ brands. That gives me
pattern-recognition most solo creators don't have.

What you'll find here:
→ Weekly breakdowns of high-performing B2B content
→ Behind-the-scenes look at content ops and workflows
→ Frameworks for building content that actually drives pipeline

I post every Tuesday and Thursday. Follow for a feed that cuts through the noise.

Topics: B2B Content Marketing, Demand Generation, SEO, Content Strategy,
Thought Leadership, LinkedIn Growth, SaaS Marketing

Want to collaborate or work together? DM me or email [email].
```

**What makes this work:** The opener states exactly what the follower gets,
and when. The credibility comes from "both sides" — experience that isn't
common. The CTA tells the follower when to expect content, reducing friction
to the Follow click.

#### Personal Branding (authority position)

```
I've spent 15 years arguing that most enterprise software is designed for
the company, not the human using it. Now I'm doing something about it.

As CPO at [Company], I lead a product org of 80 that ships tools used by
1.2M knowledge workers daily. Before that, I spent a decade at Microsoft
and Salesforce learning what "at scale" actually means.

What I write about here:
→ Why most roadmaps are lying to you (and how to fix them)
→ How to build product intuition you can't teach in an MBA
→ The real cost of shipping fast vs. shipping right

I share one post per week — usually provocative, always grounded in what
I've seen actually work in the trenches.

DM me or connect if you're building product at scale and want to compare notes.
```

**What makes this work:** The opener is a stated conviction — immediately
signals a perspective worth following. Credibility is specific and verifiable.
The content tease creates anticipation. The CTA is peer-to-peer, not
transactional — appropriate for a senior authority voice.

### 3.4 Per-Mode About Emphasis

| Mode | Hook style | Achievements frame | CTA |
|------|-----------|-------------------|-----|
| **Job search** | Mission statement: "I help [target] do [outcome]" | Metric bullets — quantified wins relevant to target role | "Open to [role type] opportunities — reach me at [email]" |
| **Consulting** | Problem hook: "If [client problem] — I fix that" | Case study results — client outcomes, not responsibilities | Book a call / email + "I work with X clients at a time" |
| **Creator** | Value-for-follower hook: "Every week I…" | Content track record + platform signal | "Follow for [frequency] posts on [topic]" |
| **Personal branding** | Conviction statement: provocative or declarative | Selective career credibility + what you stand for | Peer-to-peer invitation: "connect if you're building…" |

## 4. Experience Playbook

### 4.1 Per-Role Format

```
[Job Title] · [Company Name] · [Month Year – Month Year or Present] · [City, Country or Remote]

[Role summary: 2–3 sentences. Cover scope, team size, key mandate, and business context.
 This is the one place for a short narrative before bullets.]

• [Active verb] [result or metric] [keyword where natural]
• [Active verb] [result or metric] [keyword where natural]
• [Active verb] [result or metric] [keyword where natural]
• [Active verb] [result or metric] [keyword where natural]
• [Active verb] [result or metric] [keyword where natural]
```

**Bullet count target:** 4–6 per role. Fewer than 4 leaves value on the table;
more than 6 dilutes focus and buries the key wins.

**Verb bank — start each bullet with one of these:**

Built · Launched · Led · Grew · Drove · Increased · Reduced · Delivered · Owned ·
Designed · Scaled · Automated · Negotiated · Partnered · Shipped · Managed ·
Deployed · Established · Revamped · Closed · Hired · Coached · Published · Executed

### 4.2 Before / After Example

**CURRENT (weak prose — common pattern):**

```
Product Manager at Acme Corp (2019–2022)

Responsible for managing the product roadmap and working with engineers and
designers. Helped improve user retention and was involved in launching new
features. Worked closely with the sales team to understand customer needs.
```

**OPTIMIZED (tight metric bullets — what it should look like):**

```
Senior Product Manager · Acme Corp · Jan 2019 – Mar 2022 · San Francisco, CA

Led the B2B product line for Acme's core SaaS platform, owning the roadmap for a
team of 12 engineers and 3 designers serving 8,000+ SMB customers.

• Grew platform revenue from $5M to $20M ARR by launching 3 new product lines
  and expanding into enterprise
• Led cross-functional team of 15 (eng, design, data) to deliver 25+ features
  with 95% on-time delivery
• Improved retention from 82% to 94% through proactive feature development
  based on usage analytics
• Established product analytics framework using Amplitude, increasing feature
  adoption by 40%
• Partnered with sales to build a customer-feedback loop that reduced churn
  by 15% in 6 months
```

**What changed:**
- "Responsible for" → specific scope (team size, customer count)
- "Helped improve" → specific metric (82% → 94% retention)
- "Was involved in launching" → "Led cross-functional team of 15 to deliver 25+ features"
- "Worked closely with" → "Partnered with sales to build a feedback loop that reduced churn by 15%"
- Added dollar metrics, team sizes, and tool names (Amplitude)

### 4.3 Per-Mode Experience Emphasis

| Mode | Bullet priority | Keyword emphasis | What to amplify |
|------|----------------|-----------------|-----------------|
| **Job search** | Metric-driven bullets first; ATS-visible outcomes | Exact job-posting language; role-specific tools; level-appropriate scope | Quantified results; role title progression; keywords from target JDs |
| **Consulting** | Client outcome and ROI per bullet | Problem → solution → impact; industry verticals served | Client names (where permitted); dollar impact; transformation framing |
| **Creator / branding** | Narrative + signature wins; milestone-focused | Platform names; audience size; media mentions | Awards, publications, speaking engagements; posts / reach / builds |
| **Personal branding** | Authority-signal bullets; scope and scale | Leadership language; company / org prestige; industry context | Scale numbers; org size; named initiatives; external recognition |

## 5. Skills and Keywords Playbook

### 5.1 Skills Section Rules

| Rule | Detail |
|------|--------|
| **Maximum skills** | 50 (LinkedIn's hard cap) |
| **Top 3 pinned** | These appear on the profile card without clicking — make them your highest-value, most-searched keywords for the target role |
| **Ordering principle** | Relevance to target role first, then endorsement count; endorsed skills appear more credible in search |
| **Remove** | Outdated tools, generic soft skills without endorsements, anything irrelevant to the target role |

### 5.2 Skill Categories

Build the final skills list from these five categories, weighted toward
categories 1 and 2:

| Category | Examples | Target count |
|----------|----------|-------------|
| **Job-specific skills** | Product Management, Data Analysis, Financial Modeling, Content Strategy | 10–15 |
| **Tools and platforms** | SQL, Python, Figma, Salesforce, HubSpot, AWS, Amplitude, Tableau | 10–15 |
| **Methodologies and frameworks** | Agile, Scrum, OKRs, Lean Startup, Design Thinking, Six Sigma | 5–8 |
| **Soft skills** | Cross-functional Leadership, Stakeholder Management, Executive Communication | 3–5 |
| **Industry terms** | B2B SaaS, Enterprise Software, HealthTech, PLG, Series A–C | 3–5 |

### 5.3 Keyword Sourcing — Where to Find the Right Terms

**Source 1: Target job descriptions**
Paste 5–10 current job descriptions for the target role into a text doc.
Count repeating words and phrases. The top 10–15 are your keyword targets.
Use exact phrasing — LinkedIn search is literal, not semantic.

**Source 2: Peer profiles**
Find 5–10 profiles of people already in the target role at companies you
want to work at (or your ideal client companies). Screenshot their Skills
sections. Note the exact terms they use.

**Source 3: LinkedIn's own skill suggestions**
When editing the Skills section on LinkedIn, the platform auto-suggests
high-volume skills based on your profile text. These suggestions reflect
actual search volume — they are a direct signal.

**Source 4: Industry publications and conferences**
The vocabulary that appears in headlines of trade publications, conference
track names, and award categories signals what the industry considers
important. These often lag job descriptions by 12–18 months but confirm
longer-lasting keyword value.

**Source 5: Certifications and credentials**
If you hold a certification, list it as an exact-match skill string
(e.g., "AWS Certified Solutions Architect," "Google Analytics 4") — these
are independently searched.

### 5.4 Keyword Placement Priority

LinkedIn's search algorithm weights keyword location. Listed from
highest to lowest impact:

| Rank | Location | Why It Matters |
|------|----------|---------------|
| 1 | **Headline** | Highest search weight; appears in every surface (search results, messages, connection requests, "People Also Viewed") |
| 2 | **About section** | Second-highest weight; repeat target keywords naturally 2–3× in the body |
| 3 | **Experience — job titles** | Exact title matching is a primary recruiter filter |
| 4 | **Experience — bullet text** | Keywords in bullet descriptions add significant index weight |
| 5 | **Skills section** | Endorsements boost the signal, but placement matters even without them |
| 6 | **Recommendations** | Keywords written by others carry third-party credibility signal |

**The exact-match rule:** LinkedIn search is not semantic. "Growth Marketing"
and "growth marketer" are different strings. Use the exact phrase that
appears in job descriptions and peer profiles. Do not paraphrase keywords.
