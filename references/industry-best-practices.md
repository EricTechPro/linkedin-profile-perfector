## 1. All-Star Completeness Checklist

LinkedIn assigns profiles a completeness level that directly affects how
often they appear in search results. The highest tier is **All-Star**, and
profiles at All-Star appear significantly higher in recruiter searches than
incomplete profiles.

### 1.1 Minimum Requirements for All-Star Status

| Requirement | Details |
|-------------|---------|
| **Professional photo** | Any photo — quality matters for scoring but presence triggers the level |
| **Custom headline** | Must be edited — not left as the default "[Title] at [Company]" |
| **Current position with description** | Title, company, and at least one description line on the current role |
| **Two past positions** | At least two prior roles listed with title and company |
| **Education** | At least one education entry with institution and degree |
| **5+ skills** | LinkedIn requires a minimum of 5 skills to hit All-Star |
| **Industry** | Industry field must be selected |
| **Location** | City/region must be filled in |
| **50+ connections** | Connection count must reach 50+ |

A profile meeting all nine criteria above reaches All-Star status on
LinkedIn's internal completeness meter.

### 1.2 Beyond All-Star — What Separates Top-1% Profiles

Reaching All-Star is the floor, not the ceiling. The profiles that actually
convert — landing recruiter outreach, inbound leads, and speaking invitations
— have all of the following:

| Enhancement | Target |
|-------------|--------|
| **Custom banner image** | On-brand, reinforces value prop; not the default blue/grey |
| **Featured section populated** | At least 2–3 items: portfolio, top post, article, external link |
| **About section depth** | 1,500+ characters; hook visible in the 300-char preview |
| **Rich media in Experience** | At least one role has attached images, documents, or links |
| **500+ connections** | Triggers LinkedIn's "500+" badge; increases search weighting |
| **5+ recommendations** | Minimum for trust signal; 8–10 for consulting and executive roles |
| **All 50 skills** | Max skill count maximises endorsement surface and keyword indexing |
| **Certifications listed** | All current credentials in the Licenses & Certifications section |
| **Education detail** | Degree, field of study, activities, and societies — not just institution name |
| **Volunteer / causes** | Even one entry shows dimensionality; valued by recruiters in many industries |

**The completeness compound effect:** LinkedIn's algorithm treats each
additional completed section as a signal that the profile is trustworthy and
actively maintained. A profile with all 50 skills, 8 recommendations, a
featured section, and regular posts can appear 10× more often in search
results than an All-Star profile with no further enhancements.

## 2. Recruiter Visibility and Open-to-Work Settings

> This section is most critical for **Job Search** mode, but the visibility
> settings affect every mode — consulting profiles should also be discoverable.

### 2.1 Open-to-Work Settings

To be found by recruiters sourcing for active candidates, configure the
Open-to-Work preferences at `linkedin.com → Me → Settings → Job seeking preferences`:

| Setting | What to Enter |
|---------|---------------|
| **Job titles** | List 3–5 exact titles you're targeting. Use the same phrasing as the job descriptions you're applying to. LinkedIn uses these to match recruiter searches. |
| **Locations** | Add all acceptable locations and enable "Remote" if open. Include metro areas (not just cities) for broader matching. |
| **Start date** | "Immediately" or a specific date. Recruiters filter by availability. |
| **Job types** | Select all that apply: Full-time, Part-time, Contract, Temporary, Internship, Volunteer. Don't narrow unnecessarily. |
| **Workplace type** | On-site / Hybrid / Remote — select all that you'd genuinely accept. |

### 2.2 Open-to-Work Visibility Options

| Option | Effect | When to Use |
|--------|--------|------------|
| **All LinkedIn members** | Shows a green "Open to Work" banner on your profile photo | Job seeking is not a concern for current employer; maximises recruiter inbound |
| **Recruiters only** | No visible badge; visible only to LinkedIn Recruiter subscribers | Currently employed; want to explore discreetly without alerting employer |

**Recommendation:** If you're actively searching and employed, use "Recruiters
only" for 4–6 weeks before switching to "All members" if searches are
generating insufficient inbound. If you're not currently employed, "All
members" + the green badge raises response rate meaningfully.

### 2.3 Profile Visibility Settings

These settings control whether your profile appears in search and how
recruiters find you. Check these at `Settings & Privacy → Visibility`:

| Setting | Recommended state | Reason |
|---------|------------------|--------|
| **Profile viewing options** | Your name and headline | Recruiters are less likely to visit a profile they can't identify; anonymous viewing reduces their confidence |
| **Allow recruiters to see you're Open to Work** | Enabled (Recruiters only or All) | Required for Open-to-Work matching |
| **Visibility of your LinkedIn activity** | Connections and followers | Demonstrates activity without exposing internal moves |
| **Viewers of this profile also viewed** | On | Keeps you in the "People Also Viewed" panel — passive SEO |
| **Profile discovery using email address** | Connections | Allows known contacts to find you |
| **Show your profile when someone searches on Google** | On (for public profiles) | For job search and branding modes; enables Google indexing of your name + role |

### 2.4 Profile URL

Customize your LinkedIn URL at `Edit public profile & URL`:

- Format: `linkedin.com/in/firstname-lastname` or `firstname-lastname-role`
- A custom URL signals attention to detail and makes sharing easier
- Include in resume, email signature, and business card

## 3. Featured Section

The Featured section appears prominently on the profile — directly below
the About section on desktop. It is one of the most overlooked and highest-
return sections of the profile.

### 3.1 Why It Matters

The Featured section is the only place on LinkedIn where the profile actively
shows work rather than describing it. For every mode — job search, consulting,
creator, and personal branding — it converts profile visitors into believers
faster than any amount of text.

On desktop, Featured items appear as visual cards with thumbnails. Visitors
naturally gravitate to visual content before reading bullets.

### 3.2 What to Put in Featured

| Content type | Best for | Notes |
|-------------|----------|-------|
| **Portfolio pieces** | Creatives, designers, writers | Link to Behance, Dribbble, personal site, or PDF upload |
| **Published articles** | All modes | LinkedIn articles rank in Google; external publications with your byline |
| **Presentations and slide decks** | Consulting, personal branding | Upload as PDFs; LinkedIn renders them as swipe-through cards |
| **Media appearances** | Personal branding, consulting | Podcast episodes, video interviews, TV/press mentions |
| **Top LinkedIn posts** | Creator mode | Feature posts with high engagement — social proof in a click |
| **Case study or results doc** | Consulting | Upload a 1-page outcome summary (anonymised client if needed) |
| **Project links** | Engineering, product, marketing | GitHub, product pages, campaign landing pages |
| **Certifications or awards** | Job search, credentialing | Feature the certificate image or the announcement post |

**Minimum target:** 2–3 Featured items for any serious profile. 4–6 items
for consulting and creator modes where the section functions as a portfolio.

### 3.3 Featured Section Ordering

Order by the most likely "first thing" your target audience wants to see:

- **Recruiters** → most recent or most impressive project first
- **Consulting clients** → case study or ROI doc first
- **Followers** → most-engaged post or most recent article first
- **Media/speaking organizers** → speaker reel or published article first

## 4. Recommendations Strategy

Recommendations are the highest-trust signal on LinkedIn because they are
written by someone else, named, and publicly visible. A recommendation carries
far more weight than any self-written claim in the same profile.

### 4.1 Target Counts

| Profile stage | Minimum | Strong | Elite |
|---------------|---------|--------|-------|
| General / job search | 3 | 5–7 | 10+ |
| Consulting | 5 | 8–10 | 12+ (inc. 5+ client recs) |
| Executive / senior leadership | 5 | 7–9 | 10+ (inc. board/peer level) |
| Creator / personal brand | 2–3 | 5 | 7+ |

### 4.2 Best Sources — Ranked by Impact

| Source | Why It's Valuable |
|--------|-----------------|
| **Former managers** | Carry the most authority; they had visibility into your full performance |
| **Direct reports** | Reverse reference — shows leadership quality and trust from below |
| **Cross-functional partners** | Demonstrates collaboration across business units; valued highly by hiring managers |
| **Clients** | Highest value for consulting profiles; transforms a claim into verified proof |
| **Peers in equivalent roles** | Adds volume; slightly lower weight than manager/client voices |
| **Mentors / sponsors** | Useful for early-career profiles or niche-credentialing scenarios |

### 4.3 How to Get High-Quality Recommendations

**Step 1 — Give first.**
Write a thoughtful, specific recommendation for the person before you ask.
This is not just reciprocity — it signals that you give as much as you get,
and it gives the other person a stylistic model to follow.

**Step 2 — Ask specific people, not everyone.**
Target 5–7 people who (a) know your work well, (b) represent different
relationship types (manager, peer, client), and (c) whose own profiles
are credible. Shotgun requests to 20 people produce generic results.

**Step 3 — Make it easy with talking points.**
When you request a recommendation, give them 2–3 bullet points:
- The specific project or period you worked together
- One or two outcomes or skills you'd like them to reference
- Any keywords relevant to your current goal (they don't need to use your
  exact language, but context helps)

**Example request message:**
> "Hi [Name] — I'm refreshing my LinkedIn profile for a [job search / consulting
> push / personal brand build]. Would you be willing to write a recommendation?
> I'd especially love if you could mention the [Project X] work we did together
> and the [specific outcome] we delivered. Happy to return the favour — I already
> have a draft for you if you'd like."

**Step 4 — Time it after a win.**
The best time to ask is within 2–4 weeks of completing a successful project,
ending a role well, or closing a deal. The work is fresh, the goodwill is high,
and specifics are easy to recall.

**Step 5 — Rotate for recency.**
Recommendations older than 3–4 years carry less weight. Each year, aim to
add 1–2 new recommendations to keep the section recent.

### 4.4 What a Strong Recommendation Looks Like

**Weak (generic — adds little signal):**
> "[Name] is a hardworking professional who was always a pleasure to work with.
> I highly recommend them to any employer."

**Strong (specific — adds real proof):**
> "[Name] led the product redesign that took our B2B dashboard from a 38% DAU
> to 61% in 4 months. She ran the cross-functional team of 8 without needing
> to escalate anything to me, navigated two significant scope changes without
> slipping the date, and shipped with near-zero post-launch bugs. I'd hire her
> again immediately."

A strong recommendation names a specific project, names a specific outcome,
and communicates what it was like to work with this person under real conditions.

## 5. Content Cadence

### 5.1 Posting Frequency

| Frequency | Tier | Notes |
|-----------|------|-------|
| Daily | Elite | Top-of-algorithm placement; requires a content system to sustain |
| 3–5×/week | Optimal | Best balance of visibility and quality; algorithm rewards consistency |
| 1–2×/week | Minimum effective | Sufficient for job search; baseline for personal brand |
| 2–3×/month | Visible but weak | Profile views will be low; algorithm de-prioritises inactive accounts |
| Rarely or never | Invisible | Profile views drop to near zero; only searchability matters |

**Daily passive engagement (separate from posting):**
Commenting on 3–5 posts per day, even without posting, increases SSI score,
keeps you in connections' feeds, and builds relationships that convert to
opportunities. This is the minimum viable activity for every mode.

### 5.2 Content Types That Perform on LinkedIn

| Type | Engagement pattern | Best for |
|------|-------------------|----------|
| **Industry insight / take** | High initial spread; opinion-driven; comment-rich | Personal branding, Creator |
| **Lessons learned / mistakes made** | High relatability; strong share rate | All modes |
| **Milestone / win announcement** | High like count; signals momentum | Job search (post a new role), Consulting (close a client) |
| **How-to / framework** | Strong save rate; attracts followers | Creator, Consulting |
| **Resources / curated list** | Reliable reach; good for consistent posting | All modes |
| **Engaging with others' content** | Low direct visibility but high relationship value | All modes (daily habit) |
| **Video or document carousel** | Highest reach on LinkedIn currently; algorithm-favoured | Creator, Personal branding |
| **Newsletter / article** | Google-indexed; long shelf life; drives profile visits | All modes for long-form credibility |

### 5.3 Content Format Tips

- **Start with a hook line** — the first 2–3 words determine whether someone
  stops scrolling. Lead with curiosity, a surprising stat, a direct statement,
  or a clear promise ("3 things I learned…").
- **Short paragraphs** — one sentence or two per paragraph max. Mobile-first.
  White space is a feature, not a waste.
- **End with a question or call to action** — "What's your take?" or "Save this
  for your next job search" — prompts engagement, which the algorithm rewards.
- **Tags sparingly** — tag 2–3 people who are directly relevant (mentioned in
  the post, would genuinely benefit from seeing it). Over-tagging reduces reach.
- **Hashtags** — 3–5 targeted hashtags. Use niche ones (lower volume, higher
  relevance) over generic ones ("#leadership" is saturated).

## 6. The Recruiter 6-Second Scan

### 6.1 What It Is (Canonical Definition)

When a recruiter, hiring manager, or prospective client lands on a LinkedIn
profile — whether through search results, a referral link, or an InMail thread
— they spend approximately **6 seconds** scanning before deciding to read further
or move on.

**The 6-second scan covers exactly three surfaces:**

1. **Headline** — LinkedIn displays up to ~220 chars; treat it as read in full. This is the highest-weight surface in the decision
2. **First ~300 characters of About** — visible without clicking "see more"; scanned, not read
3. **Current role title and company** — status signal and context anchor

**The test:** "In those three surfaces, does the target audience immediately
understand who this person is, who they help, how they help them, and is
there any proof?" If yes → CLICK. If no → SKIP.

This test is run before section scoring in every audit, for every mode.

### 6.2 CLICK vs. SKIP Checklist

**SKIP triggers — any of these alone can cause a pass:**

- [ ] Headline is vague: no specific role, no value prop, no keywords ("Professional · Leader · Strategist")
- [ ] Headline uses passive language: "Seeking opportunities," "Open to work," "Aspiring [role]"
- [ ] No keywords in headline for the role/audience being targeted
- [ ] About section first line starts with the person's name or "I am a..."
- [ ] About section first 300 chars is a wall of text (no line breaks on mobile)
- [ ] About section opener is generic: "Passionate professional with 10+ years..."
- [ ] Current role is missing, listed as "Unemployed," or clearly outdated
- [ ] No differentiation — 10,000 other profiles say exactly the same thing

**CLICK triggers — the presence of even 2–3 drives engagement:**

- [ ] Specific value prop in headline: "I help [X] do [Y]" or "[Role] who [outcome]"
- [ ] Target keyword in first 5 words of headline
- [ ] Quantified proof in headline or first About line (number, metric, scale)
- [ ] Clear "who I help" framing — right audience self-selects immediately
- [ ] Social proof in the hook: "Featured in X," "Built Y," "Led team of Z"
- [ ] Short punchy first line that creates a question or gap in the reader's mind
- [ ] Active verb framing: "Building / Scaling / Leading" (signals momentum)
- [ ] Current role at a recognizable company or in an unmistakable context

### 6.3 Before / After Examples

#### Example A — Software Engineer, Job Search

**BEFORE:**

```
Headline:  "Software Engineer at [Company]"
About:     "I am a software engineer with 6 years of experience in backend
            development. I have worked on various projects using Python, Java,
            and cloud technologies. I am passionate about building scalable
            systems and am currently looking for new opportunities."
Current:   Software Engineer at [Company]
```

**6-second verdict: SKIP**

Issues:
- Headline is title-only; no differentiation from 500K other "Software Engineer" profiles
- About opens with "I am" — wastes the hook slot
- "Passionate about building scalable systems" → generic; Cut List item
- "Currently looking for new opportunities" → passivity signal in prime real estate

**AFTER:**

```
Headline:  "Senior Backend Engineer | Python, AWS, Kubernetes | Building Scalable
            Systems for 10M+ User Products"
About:     "I've shipped backend infrastructure that handles 10M+ daily requests
            without going down — even on Black Friday.

            6 years at [Company] and [Company], mostly working on the parts
            of the stack that can't afford to fail: payments, auth, real-time
            data pipelines.

            What I specialise in:
            → High-availability distributed systems
            → Python / Django / FastAPI at scale
            → AWS infrastructure (EKS, RDS, Lambda, CloudFront)

            Open to Staff / Senior Backend roles at product companies that care
            about reliability. Reach me at [email]."
Current:   Senior Backend Engineer at [Company]
```

**6-second verdict: CLICK**

What changed: Specific scale ("10M+ daily requests"), tools in headline for
ATS matching, About opener is a specific memorable claim, not a bio.

**BEFORE:**

```
Headline:  "Marketing Consultant | Helping businesses grow"

**6-second verdict: CLICK**

What changed: Headline names the specific problem and the specific client
(SaaS, Series A–C). About opener is a problem the right client recognises.
Results are specific, verifiable, and show transformation. The offer is scoped.

#### Example C — Personal Brand, Thought Leadership

**BEFORE:**

```
Headline:  "Experienced HR Professional | People & Culture | Thought Leader"
About:     "I am passionate about creating positive workplace cultures and helping
            organisations build high-performing teams. With 12 years in Human
            Resources, I have developed a deep expertise in talent acquisition,

**6-second verdict: SKIP**

Issues:
- "Thought Leader" — never self-apply; it devalues immediately
- "Passionate about creating positive workplace cultures" → generic; every HR
  person says this
- No differentiation, no point of view, no reason to follow

**AFTER:**

```

            Writing About the Future of Work"
About:     "Most companies say people are their greatest asset. Very few act like it.

            After 12 years building people functions at companies from 50 to 5,000
            employees, I've learned exactly where the gap between rhetoric and
            reality shows up — and how to close it.

            I write here every week about:
            → Why performance management as we know it doesn't work
            → What the best managers do differently (and what the research actually says)
            → The specific levers that move retention without increasing compensation

            VP People at [Company] (Series C, 400 employees). Former: [Company], [Company].

            Follow for posts every Tuesday. DM me if you're building a people
            function and want to compare notes."
Current:   VP People at [Company]
```

**6-second verdict: CLICK**

What changed: Opener is a conviction statement — memorable and shareable.
Content promise is specific (what they'll get, when). Credibility is shown
through tenure and company stage, not self-labels.

## 7. Per-Mode "What Great Looks Like"

### 7.1 Mode: Personal Branding — Build Authority

**Profile pattern:**

| Section | What great looks like |
|---------|----------------------|
| **Headline** | Conviction statement + area of authority + what you're building ("VP Engineering \| Scaling Engineering Cultures \| Writing About What Nobody Tells Junior Devs") |
| **About** | Opens with a strong point-of-view sentence. 3–5 paragraphs. Content tease in the middle ("I post every Thursday about..."). CTA is peer-to-peer ("connect if you're building..."). 1,500–2,000 chars. |
| **Experience** | Scale-signaling bullets — team size, org scope, company recognition. Narrative tone where appropriate. Signature wins named. |
| **Featured** | 3–5 items: best-performing post, top article, press mention or speaking slot, signature framework/resource. |
| **Content cadence** | 3–5 posts per week. Mix of takes, frameworks, and personal stories. Engagement in comments daily. Consistent topic territory — not scattered. |
| **Skills** | 30–50 skills. Top 3 are the keywords the target audience searches. Endorsed by relevant industry peers. |
| **Recommendations** | 5+ from peers, managers, collaborators. Specific, named outcomes. Recency within 2–3 years. |

---

### 7.2 Mode: Job Search — Land a Role

**Profile pattern:**

| Section | What great looks like |
|---------|----------------------|
| **Headline** | Exact target title first. 2–3 tool/skill keywords. Scope or metric signal at end ("Senior PM \| B2B SaaS \| 3× ARR Growth \| Open to Product Leadership Roles"). |
| **About** | Hooks with a strong mission or metric. Demonstrates the target role's core competencies with specific proof. Closes with explicit availability and role type. Keywords woven in naturally 3–5×. |
| **Experience** | Every role has 4–6 metric-driven bullets. ATS keywords embedded naturally. Titles match target role language. Clear progression visible across the section. No unexplained gaps. |
| **Featured** | Portfolio or published work that proves the competency the target role requires. Case study PDF. GitHub or project link. |
| **Open-to-Work** | Configured with exact target titles, locations, start date. Visibility set appropriately for employment situation. |
| **Skills** | 40–50 skills. Top 3 are the most-searched terms in target JDs. Missing skills from JDs identified and added. Endorsements on top 5+ skills. |
| **Recommendations** | 5+ with at least 2 from managers. At least one recent (within 18 months). References target role competencies explicitly. |

---

### 7.3 Mode: Creator / Grow Profile — Build a Following

**Profile pattern:**

| Section | What great looks like |
|---------|----------------------|
| **Headline** | Names the topic and the value for followers ("I teach B2B founders how to build content without an agency \| 40K followers \| Weekly frameworks"). |
| **About** | Value-for-follower hook in the first line. States topic clearly and posting frequency. Includes social proof (follower count, notable post, publications). Ends with Follow CTA. |
| **Experience** | Less emphasis on bullets; more narrative. Signature projects and content milestones featured. Media/press appearances listed. |
| **Featured** | Top post or video (highest engagement). Best article or newsletter issue. Collaboration highlight. Newsletter subscribe link or digital product. |
| **Content cadence** | 3–5 posts per week minimum. Consistent format or series that followers expect. Daily engagement in comments. Creator Mode enabled. |
| **Skills** | 30–50 skills with content-specific terms prominent ("Content Strategy," "Audience Growth," "LinkedIn Growth," "Newsletter Writing," platform-specific tools). |
| **Recommendations** | 3–5 from collaborators, partners, and community members who can speak to the quality and impact of the content. |

---

### 7.4 Mode: Consulting / Win Clients — Attract and Convert Leads

**Profile pattern:**

| Section | What great looks like |
|---------|----------------------|
| **Headline** | Problem + client + outcome. Who you serve, what you fix, what they get ("B2B Revenue Consultant \| GTM & Sales Systems \| Helped 40+ SaaS Companies Hit $1M ARR"). |
| **About** | Opens with the client's problem. States transformation delivered. Lists 3–5 specific client results with metrics. Scopes the offer ("I work with 4–5 clients at a time"). Ends with a booking link or email. |
| **Experience** | Every role framed as client outcomes and ROI, not internal responsibilities. Case study language ("Led engagement that..." "Delivered..."). Client-facing evidence prominent. |
| **Featured** | Case study PDF (anonymised if needed). Best testimonial or recommendation screenshot. Published article establishing expertise. Client logo wall if permitted. |
| **Services section** | LinkedIn's Services feature enabled with clear service listing, pricing range if appropriate, and service area. This appears in search. |
| **Recommendations** | 5+ with a majority from clients. Each one names a specific project and a specific result. Recency matters — at least 2 within the past 12 months. |
| **Skills** | 30–50 skills. Industry verticals served listed as skills. Methodology names prominent (Lean, OKRs, GTM, Revenue Operations). "Fractional [Role]" if applicable. |
